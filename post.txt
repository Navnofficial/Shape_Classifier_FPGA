No neural network. No ML library. Just math, hardware, and one very stubborn bug.

Everyone reaches for PyTorch the moment someone says "classifier." I went the other way — pure geometry, fixed-point arithmetic, and a Cyclone II FPGA.
Here's what I built:
The system:
Python GUI captures a hand-drawn shape. Crops it, centers it by center of mass, resizes to 28×28. Extracts 5 geometric features — area, aspect ratio, row variance, symmetry, perimeter. Packs 784 pixels into a 100-byte frame with XOR checksum. Streams over UART at 115,200 baud to Altera DE2.
On the FPGA:

uart_rx deserializes and validates the frame
feature_extractor FSM scans all 784 bits — one pixel per clock cycle — computing features in hardware
classifier — purely combinational, calculates weighted L1 distance to 4 shape centroids simultaneously, returns the winner in a single clock cycle — 20 nanoseconds

Result back to GUI in under 9 milliseconds. Displayed on 7-segment and LEDs.
Why fixed-point, not floating-point:
FPGAs have no native floating-point ALUs. Synthesizing IEEE-754 burns LUTs at a scale that makes no sense here. Instead — Q8.8 fixed-point for aspect ratio (256 = 1.0), 10-bit area, 5-bit row variance. Standard integer adders. Manhattan distance instead of Euclidean — no squaring, no square roots, no embedded multipliers.
5,326 LUTs. 16% of the chip. Zero embedded multipliers used.
The math behind classification:
Nearest-centroid with weighted L1 distance:
Distance = W_area×|Area - C_area| + W_aspect×|Aspect - C_aspect| + ...
Symmetry gets weight 4. Area gets weight 1. Calibrated from 20 hand-drawn samples per shape. The shape with the lowest total distance wins.
The bug that taught me everything:
Every shape came back as Rectangle. Circle, Triangle, Square — all Rectangle. Every time.
Not the math. Not the centroids. Not the classifier.
The Python GUI packs pixels MSB-first — pixel 0 at bit 7. The FPGA was unpacking LSB-first. Every 8-pixel chunk was mirrored. The geometric layout of the entire 28×28 grid was scrambled before it ever reached the feature extractor.
The classifier was doing its job perfectly. It was classifying the wrong data perfectly.
Found it by isolating pixel_bits in the testbench — the 784-bit output from uart_rx. Fixed by explicitly reversing the bit mapping in the unpack loop. One fix. Everything worked.
Interface bug masquerading as algorithm failure. In RTL these look identical until you trace the data path bit by bit.
One honest thing:
The timing analyzer reports -63.727 ns setup slack. The combinational classifier — all 4 distance calculations in parallel, single cycle — is too wide for 50 MHz without pipelining. It works on hardware. It shouldn't, by the numbers. Pipelining the adder tree is the next fix.
